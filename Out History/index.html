Kolhapuri Chhapals-Growing in Pride

Kolhapuri Chappal, a craft so intricate and rich that has been practiced for ages, is a beauty in itself. It is a craft the heart of which lies in the hard work and toil of its creator, the people of Bharat. Through the pages of time, this craft has slowly started loosening its grip in the world of the present. A craft so beyond perfect, has been slowly making its way through the circumstances of time. The project focuses on showing the beauty and the boundless hard work that goes into its creation. It is all about exploring the authentic craftsmanship, its composition, beauty and its journey through time.
Kolhapuri Chappal, is a story that was once loud and unheard that got feeble through the fragments of its course. It is a legacy that must be preserved.



Origin of Kolhapuri Chappal



The chappal came into existence as early as the 13th century. History has recorded that it was started and designed by the Saudagar family in 1920.

The first model which was designed earlier was much thinner than what is available currently and was also known as "Chappal with ears" or "Kanwali" for it had two side flaps. The upper portion of the footwear was intricately designed. By the 18th century, it became popularly known as "Kolhapuri".

Material Used


The following raw materials are used for making Kolhapuri Chappals:
-Mhasdi leather (Buffalo leather) - It is the most important raw material for making kolhapuri chappals and is used for the preparation of the lower sole -Shepati (leather of tails of cattles)
- For making the thread that is used to stitch the upper and lower sole
-Cotton & Nylon Threads - Used to stitch the chappals according to customers demands and also for embroidery purposes Rubber Powder - Used for sticking the leather
-Sisal Leaf (Cactus) - Sisal leaves are used to stitch the skin of cow/buffalo at the time of tanning
-Chuna (limestone) - Used for smoothening the leather in bag tanning
process
Vegetables - Used for making dyes

The two categorisation of workmen involved in the manufacturing process - tanners and cobblers, both interdependent on each other.

Tanners take raw hides from the villagers after the natural death of cattle like buffaloes and bullocks.

The process of tanning is laborious and involves the hide being stitched with long leaves, the flesh side inwards, and the bottom closed with a rope. Only after working for practically two years on the template, thickness of the leather, craftsmanship, fitting, shaping, upper finishing, making and colouring of the entire finished product, is the apprentice accepted as skilled.

For chappal making, skilled workers sit in a line on the floor with their legs folded in a lotus position, their work table consisting of a thick stone or wooden slab, resembling a human assembly line
